# 09 — Zentangle in Therapy and Education

> A primer on how Zentangle is being used by therapists, teachers, hospitals, and care facilities — what the evidence does and does not say (as of May 2026), and how to introduce it responsibly.

---

## Why therapists and teachers reach for Zentangle

Zentangle was never designed as a clinical intervention. It was designed in 2003–2005 by Rick Roberts and Maria Thomas as a contemplative art practice with eight ritualised steps and a deliberately small kit (a 3.5-inch tile, a Micron 01 pen, a graphite pencil). But the same features that make it accessible — the small surface, the unrepeatable strokes, the absence of "right" outcomes — have made it unusually attractive to clinicians and educators looking for a low-barrier entry into mindful art. You can hand someone a tile and a pen in five minutes; you cannot do that with watercolour, ceramics, or printmaking.

Two further properties matter. First, Zentangle has structure (steps, string, repeated pattern) but no judgement — it scaffolds activity for people who freeze when handed a blank page. Second, the slow, deliberate stroke length (2–4 seconds per line) lines up with the breath-paced movement that mindfulness research associates with parasympathetic activation. That puts Zentangle in the same neighbourhood as Jon Kabat-Zinn's Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR), but in a tactile, output-producing form rather than a seated meditation.

## Therapy applications

### Anxiety and stress

The most consistent finding in the small but growing research base is that Zentangle reduces self-reported anxiety in non-clinical adults. The deliberate stroke pace and the requirement to attend to one short pen-line at a time produce something close to an attentional anchor — similar in mechanism to MBSR body scans. Several pilot RCTs report effect sizes in the moderate range for short courses (4–8 weekly sessions of 60–90 minutes). The state shift most participants describe is not euphoria but a return to baseline calm: lower heart-rate, slower breathing, the kind of attentional narrowing that interrupts rumination.

Practically, therapists using Zentangle for anxiety tend to favour:

- 20–40 minute "tile sessions" inside a longer therapy hour
- A single tangle pattern at a time (not the full 8-step ceremony)
- Instructing clients to stop at the shading step if grounding is the goal — shading shifts the brain into evaluation mode

### Trauma recovery and PTSD

The American Art Therapy Association (AATA) is clear that Zentangle is **not** art therapy in the credentialed sense — art therapy is a master's-level licensed practice. But registered art therapists (ATR-BC) increasingly use Zentangle as a low-stakes warm-up because of three properties relevant to trauma work: it is bounded (small tile, defined edges), it requires no representational drawing (no "draw your trauma" prompt), and it produces something that looks competent on the first try, which matters for clients whose self-image has collapsed.

Programs working with veterans illustrate the model. The Mattie Kelly Arts Foundation's Warrior Arts program, launched in 2016 in the Florida panhandle, partners with Okaloosa and Bay County Vet Centers and the EOD Warrior Foundation to run recurring Zentangle workshops both in-person and virtually. The framing language is deliberately not therapeutic: workshops are "creative opportunities that promote mindfulness and resilience," and the operating principle borrowed from Zentangle's own mantra is "no mistakes, only mis-tangles." For veterans with traumatic brain injury or hyper-vigilance, the absence of failure conditions matters as much as the meditative effect.

### ADHD and executive function

A persistent informal finding (less well-studied than the anxiety work) is that adults and children with ADHD often report that Zentangle is one of the few sit-still activities they can sustain. The hypothesis is that the practice provides exactly the level of structured novelty that under-stimulated executive systems need: small, decision-rich, low-stakes, with constant micro-feedback (the line you just drew). Whether this rises to a clinical effect or is simply preferred-activity bias has not been settled in the literature, but occupational therapists working with ADHD frequently include it in their toolkit.

### Depression

Two RCTs are particularly worth flagging here:

- Chan et al. (2023) ran a randomised waitlist-controlled trial of the original Zentangle method in older adults with depressive symptoms and reported significant reductions in depression scores post-intervention. (PMC10161183)
- The same research group's earlier pilot RCT on affective well-being among adults found significant reductions in negative affect at both immediate and 2-week follow-up (DOI: 10.5014/ajot.2022.049113).

The plausible mechanism for depression is different from the mechanism for anxiety: Zentangle produces a finished object in 30 minutes. That completion-and-agency loop — "I sat down, I made something, it exists" — is the opposite of the anhedonic stuckness depression imposes. Therapists describe this as a "small win" intervention that builds behavioural activation evidence.

### Chronic pain

Pain centres in the US and UK have introduced Zentangle as part of multi-modal chronic pain programs alongside pacing education, gentle movement, and CBT for pain. The mechanism here is attentional displacement rather than mood lifting: focused absorption in a low-difficulty motor task can reduce the perceptual bandwidth available for pain signalling, similar to the gate-control rationale behind distraction-based interventions. The evidence is preliminary and almost all of it is uncontrolled.

### Oncology nursing — and a notable nurse-focused study

Most discussions of Zentangle in healthcare focus on patients. A 2024 study by Stillpoint et al. published in the Clinical Journal of Oncology Nursing (Volume 28, Issue 2, pp. 173–180; DOI: 10.1188/24.CJON.173-180) flipped the lens and looked at oncology nurses. After Zentangle sessions used as a meditative break during shifts, nurses reported significant improvements in well-being, socialisation, anxiety, fatigue, secondary traumatic stress, and overall stress. This study matters strategically: it places Zentangle inside a nurse-burnout intervention category that hospital systems will pay for.

## Academic studies — three you can cite

The research base on Zentangle is small, mostly conducted in the last six years, and dominated by Hong Kong, Taiwan, mainland China, and Australia. Quality varies. These three are the most defensible citations:

1. **Chan, Lo, Ng, Lam, Lo, Suen (2022).** *The Effects of Zentangle® on Affective Well-Being Among Adults: A Pilot Randomized Controlled Trial.* American Journal of Occupational Therapy. DOI: 10.5014/ajot.2022.049113. PubMed: 35943847. Finding: 8-week Zentangle program, n≈84, significant reductions in negative affect at post-intervention and at 2-week follow-up; participants who practiced more than 80 minutes per week showed significant reductions in anxiety symptoms and improvements in self-compassion.

2. **Chan et al. (2023).** *Effects of the original Zentangle method on older adults with depressive symptoms: a randomized waitlist-controlled trial.* PubMed Central: PMC10161183. Finding: significant reduction in depressive symptoms in the Zentangle arm versus waitlist; participants also reported improved cognitive acuity and reduced anxiety.

3. **Lo, Ho, Chan (2023).** *Exploring Zentangle as a virtual mindfulness-based art intervention for people with serious mental illness.* Frontiers in Psychiatry, 14:1260937. PMC10720359. Finding: 8 weekly 90-minute virtual sessions; preliminary evidence of positive impact on overall psychiatric symptoms and self-reported mindfulness in adults with diagnosed serious mental illness.

Two further useful citations:

- **Stillpoint et al. (2024)** in Clinical Journal of Oncology Nursing (DOI: 10.1188/24.CJON.173-180) — Zentangle as a wellness intervention for oncology nurses.
- **Usman et al. (2024)** in Brain and Behavior (DOI: 10.1002/brb3.3628) — neural correlates of Zentangle on cognitive focus, emotional well-being and stress.

For an evolving citation list, the Zentangle company curates studies at zentangle.com/pages/studies-and-stories.

## Education applications

### K–12 schools

Teachers in elementary and middle schools use Zentangle for three things: focus before a difficult lesson (the "settling tile"), fine-motor practice (especially the 02 and 005 Micron pens for older students), and integration into art curricula as a unit on pattern, repetition, and tessellation. The most reliable use case is the 10-minute focusing tile at the start of an afternoon block. Several public school systems in the United States and Hong Kong have integrated Zentangle into Social-Emotional Learning (SEL) programming; the evidence is anecdotal but strongly positive from teachers.

### Higher education and medical schools

Arts-based observation training has become widespread in medical education over the last decade. The University of Pennsylvania's Perelman School of Medicine has run an "Art, Observation, and Empathy" course built on the Project Zero framework from Harvard. While that course uses gallery-based Visual Thinking Strategies rather than Zentangle, several smaller medical schools — including the American University of the Caribbean — have piloted meditative drawing (with Zentangle as the most common variant) as an anxiety-reduction intervention for medical students preparing for boards. The evidence here is preliminary and uncontrolled but face-valid.

Art schools and design programs more frequently use Zentangle as a way to teach pattern theory, mark-making discipline, and the difference between drawing-as-output and drawing-as-process.

### Special education

Zentangle has shown adaptability for students on the autism spectrum and for learners with ADHD or specific learning differences. Three features make it fit:

- The repetition is regulating, not anxiety-producing
- The "string" — a light pencil line dividing the tile — can be drawn by the teacher to scale difficulty
- There is no requirement to draw a thing, which removes the representational pressure that often blocks learners with motor-planning differences

Special-education adaptations include using larger tiles (5×5 inch instead of 3.5-inch), pre-printed strings, and limiting tangle vocabulary to three patterns at a time.

## Senior and dementia care

This is the area where Zentangle's "everyone is an artist" framing matters most. Maria Thomas designed several simple tangles — Crescent Moon, Hollibaugh, Tipple — that require only basic motor control and that produce visually competent results immediately. Care facilities in the US, UK, Hong Kong, Taiwan, and Australia have integrated Zentangle into:

- Memory-care activity programming (typically 20–30 minute sessions, simple two-pattern combinations)
- Stroke and motor-rehabilitation occupational therapy
- Hospice and palliative-care creative programs

For residents with mid-stage dementia, Zentangle's structure is doing the cognitive scaffolding the resident can no longer do themselves: the string sets the boundary, the tangle gives a repeatable instruction, and the small tile gives a clear "done" signal. Anecdotally, facilities report reduced afternoon agitation in memory units that run a daily Zentangle session — though no controlled trial has yet been published on this specific application.

The 2024 Tandfonline study by Ho et al. on adults aged 72–100 in an Australian nursing home during COVID visitation restrictions (DOI: 10.1080/01924788.2024.2317026) reported improvements in anxiety, happiness, and manual dexterity over an 8-week course, supporting the senior-care use case.

## Practical guidance for therapists, teachers, and clinicians introducing Zentangle

**Introduce without "talent" framing.** The single most damaging opening line is "you don't have to be artistic." It activates the very evaluation the practice is designed to bypass. The Zentangle company's own framing — "no mistakes, only mis-tangles" — does the work. Better still: just demonstrate the four dots and corner-to-corner border and start drawing.

**Adapt to the population.**
- Anxiety/depression adults: keep sessions to 30–40 minutes, full 8-step ritual.
- ADHD: shorten to 15-minute "warm-ups," allow movement breaks.
- Trauma: avoid metaphorical prompts ("draw what you feel"). Stay literal — pen, line, pattern.
- Memory care: pre-draw the string, limit to two tangle patterns, large tile.
- Children (under 10): use a 5×5 tile, allow colour, drop the shading step.

**Position correctly with clients.** Zentangle is an *adjunct*. It is not psychotherapy. It is not a substitute for trauma-focused CBT, EMDR, medication, or a licensed art therapist's care plan. For severe symptoms or active crisis, refer. Frame Zentangle as a between-session practice or a within-session grounding tool, not a treatment.

**Materials matter less than ritual.** A consistent kit (same tile size, same pen, same opening sequence) produces the conditioning effect that helps clients drop into the calm state quickly. Switching tools each session breaks that conditioning.

**Don't rescue mistakes.** When a client misdraws a line, the correct response is to model treating it as a feature, not a flaw. This is the central transferable lesson of the practice.

## Evidence base summary — what to claim, what not to claim

The honest summary as of May 2026:

- **Defensible claims:** Zentangle reduces self-reported anxiety, negative affect, and stress in adults; produces measurable improvements in depressive symptoms in older adults in at least two RCTs; is well-tolerated and engaging across populations from children to dementia patients; is a useful low-barrier entry to mindful art practices.
- **Plausible but under-evidenced:** Effects on chronic pain, ADHD focus, PTSD symptoms, and clinical depression in younger adults. There are positive reports but no large controlled trials.
- **Not yet supported:** Claims of clinical superiority over other mindfulness or art-based interventions. Claims of effect on serious mental illness beyond preliminary symptom reduction. Any claim that Zentangle "treats" a diagnosis.

The current research base is small (most n's are under 100), mostly conducted by overlapping author groups in Hong Kong and Australia, and mostly compared against waitlist rather than active comparator. The findings are consistent in direction but the field needs larger, multi-site, active-comparator trials before stronger clinical claims are warranted.

For therapists and educators, the operational summary is: **Zentangle is an unusually accessible, low-risk adjunct with a defensible mechanism (mindful attention) and a small but consistent positive evidence base.** Use it as a tool, not as a treatment, and most populations and settings will benefit.
