# Competitions and Exhibitions in the Zentangle World

## A Different Kind of Art Culture: Why There Are No Official Zentangle Competitions

Before we map the events, challenges, and gatherings that animate the global Zentangle community, one fact must be stated clearly: **Zentangle, as an art form and as an organization, has no official competitions.** There is no "Zentangle of the Year" award. There is no juried prize circuit. There is no leaderboard.

This is not an oversight. It is the deliberate philosophical posture of co-founders Rick Roberts and Maria Thomas, who built the Zentangle Method on a foundation of process over outcome, presence over performance, and inclusion over hierarchy. From the earliest days, Rick and Maria have repeated a principle that has become almost a creed within the community: *anything is possible, one stroke at a time.* Competition implies a winner — and therefore many losers. The Zentangle Method, by design, refuses that frame.

The official Zentangle position is that the value of a tile lies in the act of drawing it, the breath you took while making each stroke, and the small ritual of pausing to appreciate what appeared on the paper. Comparing tiles to determine which one is "better" undermines that intention. As Rick and Maria have phrased it, every tile is "a deliberate work of art," and a deliberate work of art needs no rival.

This stance shapes everything downstream. Instead of contests, the community has cultivated a rich ecosystem of **challenges, prompts, project packs, exhibitions, and reunions** — all of which welcome participation without ranking participants. The result is one of the most genuinely inclusive art communities in existence. Children, retirees, professional illustrators, and total beginners share the same Instagram hashtags and Facebook groups, and nobody is told they don't belong.

This page maps that ecosystem. Read it as a guide to participation, not a leaderboard.

## The Official Channel: Project Pack Series

In place of a contest schedule, Zentangle HQ runs the **Project Pack** series — themed, multi-day creative projects released as kits combining materials, instruction videos, and a guided sequence of tiles that build into a finished project.

Project Packs are the closest thing the official Zentangle organization has to "events." Each one runs for a specific window during which Rick, Maria, and members of the Zentangle family release daily videos walking participants through the project. Tanglers around the world tangle along in real time, share their work-in-progress on social media, and form an informal global cohort.

As of mid-2026, **Project Pack No. 29** is the current release, with daily video lessons that began on April 3, 2026. **Project Pack No. 28**, released for the December 2025 holiday season, was titled "12 Days of Zentangle 2025" and continued the long-running tradition of an end-of-year project. The series began with **Project Pack No. 01: Role Reversal**, which introduced the concept of working with white ink on black tiles.

The series is nearing thirty entries, and each pack remains available on zentangle.com after its initial release window — meaning new tanglers can work through them at any pace. There is no deadline, no judging, and no winners.

## The Major Community Challenges

Outside the official organization, three community-run challenges have shaped the rhythm of the Zentangle world for over a decade.

### The Diva Challenge

Founded in 2010 by Laura Harms (CZT) on her blog *I Am The Diva*, the **Diva Challenge** posts a weekly tangle prompt every Monday morning. Participants tangle a tile in response, share it online, and link back. Laura ran the challenge personally from 2010 to 2017, after which the format has been continued by community members. As of 2026, Diva-style weekly prompts continue to circulate, both on Laura's blog and through community-led iterations across Facebook and dedicated tangle blogs. The Diva Challenge's longevity makes it the historical heart of the global tangle-prompt culture.

### IAST — It's A String Thing

Run by CZT Adele Bruno on her blog *Tickled to Tangle*, **IAST (It's A String Thing)** ran weekly from August 2013 through 2019, reaching 302 numbered challenges before Adele stepped back from active moderation. Each prompt provided a *string* — the abstract pencil shape that divides a tile into sections — plus a list of suggested tangles. Although IAST is no longer accepting new submissions, its archive remains online as a teaching resource: tanglers today still work through the back catalog as a structured course of study. IAST is widely cited as the gold standard for how a community challenge can be designed.

### TanglePatterns Strings (TPS)

On Linda Farmer's **TanglePatterns.com**, the weekly **TanglePatterns Strings** prompt pairs a numbered string with a small selection of tangle patterns drawn from Linda's vast indexed library. TPS skews toward the educational — it is designed to drive tanglers into Linda's pattern dictionary and reinforce learning of less-familiar tangles. It remains active as one of the most reliable weekly prompts in the community.

### Square One: Purely Zentangle

Founded by CZTs Jenny Perruzzi and Chris Titus, the **Square One: Purely Zentangle** Facebook group enforces a single rule: classic Zentangle only. White tiles, black ink, graphite shading. No color, no ZIA (Zentangle-Inspired Art), no embellishment. The group runs a weekly focus on one tangle pattern that members must include in their tile. The discipline of working within these constraints attracts both rigorous beginners and seasoned CZTs who want to return to first principles. Daily activity in the group is high, and its archive functions as a living gallery of the classic format.

## ZenAgain — The Annual Reunion

While not a competition, the most significant **annual event** in the Zentangle world is **ZenAgain**, the official reunion gathering for Certified Zentangle Teachers (CZTs).

The most recent ZenAgain took place in April 2025 at the Mystic Marriott in Mystic, Connecticut, drawing 396 CZTs in person and online. The event combined classes taught by senior CZTs, presentations from Rick and Maria, social gatherings, and a celebration of the worldwide CZT family — which now exceeds 3,975 teachers across 46 countries.

The next ZenAgain is scheduled for **October 24–27, 2027**, again in Mystic. Note that there is **no ZenAgain in 2026**; the event is held on a multi-year cadence rather than annually. The Zentangle Academy online platform, developed for hybrid attendance, allows CZTs unable to travel to participate via livestream.

ZenAgain is restricted to CZTs, but its spirit — gathering, sharing, no judging — captures the broader culture of the entire community.

## Country-Level Events and Exhibitions

Although the official organization is centered in the United States, vibrant regional scenes have grown in Asia, Europe, and beyond.

### Japan

Japan hosts one of the most active Zentangle communities outside the U.S. CZTs across Tokyo, Osaka, Yokohama, and Kyoto run regular exhibitions, often coordinated through individual CZT studios or local arts venues. CZT Tomomi Galeano is among the visible faces of the Japanese community, with an active YouTube presence. Japanese exhibitions tend to be intimate gallery shows where tanglers present completed tiles and ZIA pieces; they emphasize craft and presentation. Japan has also produced multiple Japanese-language Zentangle publications.

### Korea

The Korean Zentangle community runs studio-based exhibitions and group shows organized through CZT-led networks. Korean tanglers are particularly active on Instagram and KakaoTalk-based community channels, and ZIA work from Korea often integrates traditional brushwork sensibility.

### China and Taiwan

Mainland China and Taiwan together host numerous CZTs who curate ZIA exhibitions through individual studios. In Taiwan, **Sarah (CZT #17, of sarah-henna.com)**, based in Keelung, has taught more than 1,000 students and runs both Zentangle and Henna programs. Taiwanese CZTs frequently partner with municipal cultural centers, libraries, and adult education programs to mount group shows. Mainland China has seen ZIA appear in mixed-media gallery contexts as well.

### Europe

Europe hosts diverse Zentangle activity. Germany has been an early hub, with CZT-led seminars dating back to the early 2010s. The Netherlands, France, Italy, Spain, and the United Kingdom each have active CZT networks running regional meetups, in-person classes, and pop-up exhibitions. **SpringTangle 2026**, hosted by CZT Angie Gittles on April 25–26, 2026, exemplifies the format: a weekend gathering of online classes and community sessions open to tanglers worldwide.

### Mixed-Media and Cross-Disciplinary Shows

Beyond Zentangle-specific events, ZIA frequently appears in **broader mixed-media festivals, journaling and bullet-journaling expos, calligraphy shows, and meditation/mindfulness conferences.** Many tanglers exhibit ZIA work inside larger arts ecosystems where Zentangle becomes one technique among several.

## Why This Culture Is Valuable

It is worth pausing to consider why the no-competition stance has produced such a healthy community.

**It removes ego.** When there is no winner, the work is no longer about you. The work is about the work. Tanglers find this freeing rather than diminishing.

**It creates inclusion.** A beginner who has tangled for one week can post a tile in Square One next to a CZT with twelve years of experience, and both posts are received as art. There is no skill threshold for participation.

**It preserves the meditative function.** The moment "winning" enters the room, the practice stops being meditation and becomes performance. Rick and Maria's refusal to introduce competition has protected the practice from this drift.

**It welcomes experimentation.** Without the pressure to produce a winning piece, tanglers feel free to try unfamiliar tangles, awkward strings, and odd compositions. The community grows through this experimentation.

**It scales globally.** A culture of comparison-free sharing translates across languages and time zones in a way that prize-driven competition does not.

## How to Participate Without Competing

If you are new to Zentangle and want to plug into community activity, here is a practical sequence of entry points.

1. **Pick up the current Project Pack.** Visit zentangle.com/collections/project-packs and order the most recent kit. Tangle along with the daily videos. You'll be working alongside thousands of other tanglers in real time.

2. **Join Square One: Purely Zentangle on Facebook.** Read the group rules, watch the weekly tangle focus, and post a tile when you feel ready. The community is welcoming and answers beginner questions kindly.

3. **Subscribe to the Diva Challenge feed.** Follow Laura Harms's blog or community-curated Diva Challenge channels for the Monday prompt. Sit with it, tangle a tile, post or simply enjoy your own response.

4. **Try a TanglePatterns String.** Visit TanglePatterns.com weekly. The TPS prompts will drive you to learn new tangles you might otherwise miss.

5. **Work the IAST archive.** Even though IAST is no longer adding new prompts, its 302 archived challenges form one of the best self-paced courses in Zentangle. Pick #001 and start.

6. **Attend a regional CZT class or weekend event.** Many CZTs in your country run beginner classes; CZT-hosted weekend gatherings like SpringTangle welcome non-CZTs.

7. **If you become a CZT yourself**, you become eligible for ZenAgain — though even before that, you can participate in CZT-public events and online seminars.

The key reframe is this: in the Zentangle world, "participation" replaces "competition." You enter not to win but to make, share, see what others made, and return to the page tomorrow. That is the entire point — and the entire reward.
