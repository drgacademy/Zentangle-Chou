# 04 — Tangles Dictionary

## Introduction: The Culture of Naming Tangles

A "tangle" is a structured pattern, drawn from a small set of repeated strokes, that can be deployed to fill any region of a Zentangle tile. The Zentangle method has been called "deconstructed pattern drawing" — every tangle, no matter how complex it looks, breaks down into a simple sequence of two to six elemental strokes (line, dot, circle, curve, arc, S-curve).

The naming culture is part of the charm. Rick Roberts and Maria Thomas, the founders, name many of their original tangles after family members, places they have travelled, or simply how the pattern feels in the hand. **Hollibaugh** is named after Maria's daughter, Molly Hollibaugh (now also a Certified Zentangle Teacher and the company's lead designer). **Chillon** is named after the medieval Swiss castle. **Zinger** is named for its springy energy. **Mooka** was inspired by a poster of an Alphonse Mucha lithograph.

When other artists invent tangles and submit them to the community — most often through Linda Farmer's site **TanglePatterns.com** — they typically follow the same naming spirit: a short, evocative, often whimsical word. There is no central authority that approves a tangle as "official"; the practice is genuinely a folk tradition. A pattern becomes part of the working vocabulary because practitioners use it.

What follows is a working dictionary of more than forty classic tangles. Each entry gives the name, its likely Chinese rendering, the creator(s), a verbal description of how it is drawn, and where it was first published. **The descriptions are intended as written instruction only**; they paraphrase the public step-outs in Linda Farmer's archive and in the published Zentangle books, but no images are reproduced.

## How the Dictionary Is Organised

Tangles are grouped by their primary creator family:

- **Section A** — Official Zentangle tangles by Rick Roberts, Maria Thomas, and the Roberts/Thomas family (Molly Hollibaugh, Martha Huggins).
- **Section B** — Tangles by Sandy Steen Bartholomew (Beez in the Belfry studio).
- **Section C** — Tangles by Linda Farmer (founder of TanglePatterns.com).
- **Section D** — Tangles by other Certified Zentangle Teachers (CZTs) and notable contributors.

Within each section, tangles are listed in approximately the order in which most students encounter them.

## Section A — Official Zentangle (Roberts / Thomas family)

### 1. Crescent Moon — 新月 / 弦月紋

- **Creator:** Rick Roberts & Maria Thomas
- **First published:** Original Zentangle teaching materials; the canonical "first tangle" taught in every CZT class.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Along an edge of a section, draw a row of half-moon shapes (small bumps) facing inward.
  2. Fill each half-moon solid black.
  3. Trace a thin line just outside each black bump, following its curve — this is the first "aura."
  4. Add a second aura outside the first, then a third, until the section is filled.
- **Why it matters:** Crescent Moon teaches the foundational concept of *aura* — drawing a line that mirrors and offsets the previous shape — which appears in dozens of other tangles.

### 2. Hollibaugh — 哈利堡 / 木條交錯紋

- **Creator:** Maria Thomas (named after her daughter, Molly Hollibaugh)
- **First published:** Early Zentangle materials; featured in the *Zentangle Primer Vol. 1*.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw a long band (two parallel curves with rounded ends) crossing the section.
  2. Draw a second band that appears to pass *behind* the first — when its line meets the first band, lift the pen and resume on the other side.
  3. Continue adding bands at varied angles, each one weaving behind the previous ones.
  4. Fill the negative spaces between the bands with solid black.
- **Why it matters:** Hollibaugh introduces the technique of "drawing behind" — the most reliable way to create the illusion of depth.

### 3. Printemps — 春天紋 / 蝸旋紋

- **Creator:** Maria Thomas
- **First published:** Original Zentangle deck; *One Zentangle a Day* Day 1.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Start with a small dot or tight curl in the centre of where the spiral will be.
  2. Spiral outward in a continuous curve, keeping the gap between coils roughly even.
  3. End the spiral with a small hook or curve.
  4. Pack many spirals tightly into the section, leaving small triangular gaps between them.
- **Why it matters:** Printemps is among the most calming of all tangles — its repetitive spiralling rhythm is highly meditative.

### 4. Cadent — 對偶迴環紋

- **Creator:** Rick Roberts & Maria Thomas
- **First published:** *Zentangle Primer Vol. 1*.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Lightly mark a grid of dots on the section.
  2. Connect every adjacent pair of dots with a curve that bows outward — first all the horizontal connections, then all the verticals.
  3. The result is a network of concave-sided diamond shapes.
  4. Inside each diamond, draw a small circle, dot, or filled black centre.
- **Why it matters:** Cadent demonstrates how a hidden grid can create order without imposing rigidity.

### 5. Mooka — 慕卡藤蔓紋

- **Creator:** Maria Thomas (inspired by Alphonse Mucha's Art Nouveau lithographs)
- **First published:** *Zentangle Primer Vol. 1*; widely featured in Maria's tile demos.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Start with a long, gentle S-curve as the main stem.
  2. From the stem, sprout teardrop-shaped lobes that curl back toward the stem.
  3. Each lobe is drawn as a continuous "lasso" — out and back to the stem.
  4. Add smaller secondary lobes inside or along the main lobes for depth.
- **Why it matters:** Mooka is the canonical organic, flowing tangle — opposite in feeling to a strict grid pattern.

### 6. Florz — 地磚紋

- **Creator:** Rick Roberts & Maria Thomas
- **First published:** Early Zentangle deck; *One Zentangle a Day*.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Lay down a grid of dots.
  2. Connect each dot to its diagonal neighbours, forming a lattice of small squares set on point (diamonds).
  3. Where four diamond corners meet, place a small black diamond or filled dot.
  4. Optionally, place a small dot in the centre of each large diamond.
- **Why it matters:** Florz is the simplest grid tangle and a first step into rhythmic, tile-like patterns.

### 7. 'Nzeppel — 義式麵餃紋

- **Creator:** Rick Roberts & Maria Thomas (named for an Italian dumpling)
- **First published:** *Zentangle Primer Vol. 1*.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw a loose grid of horizontal and vertical lines at irregular intervals.
  2. Inside each cell of the grid, draw a fat pillow shape that almost — but not quite — touches all four sides.
  3. The thin gaps between pillows form a soft, pebbly grid.
  4. Optionally, shade one side of each pillow.
- **Why it matters:** The pillows give 'Nzeppel a tactile, dimensional feel without explicit shading.

### 8. Tipple — 圓珠紋

- **Creator:** Rick Roberts & Maria Thomas
- **First published:** Early Zentangle deck.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Fill the section with circles of varying sizes.
  2. Pack the larger circles first; let smaller circles fill the gaps; let very small circles fill the gaps between the gaps.
  3. Optionally shade one edge of each circle to give a bubble appearance.
- **Why it matters:** Tipple is the universal "filler" — used wherever a section needs gentle texture without competing with the main tangles.

### 9. Ahh — 啊光紋 / 星芒紋

- **Creator:** Rick Roberts & Maria Thomas
- **First published:** Early Zentangle deck.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw a small "Y" or three-rayed star.
  2. Add a fourth and fifth ray to make a small radiating burst.
  3. Repeat as scattered bursts across the section, varying size.
  4. Add small dots between the bursts as breathing space.
- **Why it matters:** Ahh feels like little exhalations on the page — its name reflects the relief sound.

### 10. Bales — 包袱紋 / 蓮葉紋

- **Creator:** Rick Roberts & Maria Thomas
- **First published:** Original Zentangle deck.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Lay down a square grid.
  2. In each square, draw two arcs that bow outward from opposite corners — they meet in the centre to form a four-petalled cushion shape.
  3. Adjacent squares' arcs connect smoothly, so the whole section reads as a quilt of soft cushions.
  4. Add dots or shading at the petal centres.
- **Why it matters:** Bales is one of the gentlest grid tangles; the curves take the rigidity out of the structure.

### 11. Static — 靜電紋 / 鋸齒交錯紋

- **Creator:** Rick Roberts & Maria Thomas
- **First published:** Early Zentangle deck.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw a vertical line.
  2. From it, project a row of zigzag lines like lightning bolts.
  3. Draw a second vertical line; project zigzags toward the first set, interlocking like teeth.
  4. Repeat in alternating columns; fill the inner triangles solid black if desired.
- **Why it matters:** Static introduces high-contrast, energetic patterns — good for generating visual punch.

### 12. Onamato — 鳴音紋 / 聲響紋

- **Creator:** Maria Thomas (a play on "onomatopoeia")
- **First published:** *Zentangle Primer Vol. 1*.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw a row of touching circles along a baseline.
  2. Draw a second row of touching circles offset above the first.
  3. Trace each circle with a thin aura just inside its edge.
  4. Fill the small interstitial spaces (where three circles meet) solid black.
- **Why it matters:** Onamato shows how aura-inside (rather than aura-outside) creates depth.

### 13. Knightsbridge — 棋盤格紋

- **Creator:** Rick Roberts & Maria Thomas
- **First published:** Original Zentangle deck.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw a grid of squares.
  2. Fill alternating squares with solid black, like a chequerboard.
  3. Optionally rotate the squares 45° to create a diamond chequer.
- **Why it matters:** Knightsbridge is the textbook "anchor" tangle — a pure black-and-white block of contrast that makes neighbouring tangles look more complex.

### 14. Sez — 切疊紋

- **Creator:** Maria Thomas
- **First published:** *Zentangle Primer Vol. 1*.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw a row of upward-pointing triangles along a baseline.
  2. Above them, draw a second row of upward-pointing triangles that sit between the first row's peaks.
  3. Continue building rows; the result is a tessellation of stacked triangles.
  4. Fill alternate triangles solid black.
- **Why it matters:** Sez is a quick way to create the illusion of woven or scaled texture.

### 15. Pokeleaf (Pokeroot) — 戳葉紋 / 小球葉紋

- **Creator:** Maria Thomas
- **First published:** *Zentangle Primer Vol. 1*.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw a curving stem.
  2. From the stem, sprout round, balloon-like leaves at intervals.
  3. Each leaf is closed off with a small notched indent at its base where it joins the stem.
  4. Add fine veins or shade one side of each leaf.
- **Why it matters:** Pokeleaf is the canonical organic tangle for vines, borders, and round leaves.

### 16. Beelight — 蜂光紋 / 蜂巢光紋

- **Creator:** Sandy Steen Bartholomew (often classified with official tangles due to ubiquity)
- **First published:** *Yoga for Your Brain*.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw a hexagonal or rounded honeycomb cell.
  2. Inside it, draw a curving "flame" shape.
  3. Add a second cell above or beside, with its own flame leaning toward the previous one.
  4. Fill the surrounds black or with hatching.
- **Why it matters:** Beelight bridges floral and geometric tangles.

### 17. Fescu — 細草紋 / 草尖紋

- **Creator:** Maria Thomas
- **First published:** Early Zentangle deck.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. From a baseline, draw a thin J-shaped curve ending in a small filled dot.
  2. Draw another J-curve beside it, bending in the same general direction but at a different angle.
  3. Continue along the section; the tangle reads as wind-blown grass.
  4. The dots at the tips are the signature feature.
- **Why it matters:** Fescu is wonderful as a border or filler against a heavier neighbour.

### 18. Shattuck — 沙塔克 / 弧網紋

- **Creator:** Maria Thomas (named after a place)
- **First published:** *Zentangle Primer Vol. 1*.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Across the section, draw a wavy horizontal line.
  2. Below it, draw a series of vertical arcs hanging from the wave like swags.
  3. Below the arcs, draw a second wavy line that mirrors the first.
  4. Continue stacking; fill inside the arcs solid black.
- **Why it matters:** Shattuck creates strong horizontal rhythm — visually like a row of black hanging fruit.

### 19. Zinger — 彈簧紋 / 飛鏢紋

- **Creator:** Maria Thomas
- **First published:** *Zentangle Primer Vol. 1*.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw a curving stem like a comma.
  2. Add a small spiral or curl at one end.
  3. Decorate the stem with parallel hatch marks or a small leaf.
  4. Scatter many Zingers in different orientations across the section.
- **Why it matters:** Zinger is technically an "embellishment" — a flourish that dresses up other tangles.

### 20. Paradox — 悖論紋 / 旋角紋

- **Creator:** Rick Roberts
- **First published:** Original Zentangle teaching materials; widely demonstrated by Rick.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Start with a triangle (or any equilateral-ish polygon).
  2. From each vertex, draw a straight line toward — but not all the way to — a point on the adjacent side, then continue inward.
  3. Each new line creates a smaller triangle inside; spiral inward.
  4. The illusion of a swirling vortex emerges from straight lines alone.
- **Why it matters:** Paradox is one of the most visually striking tangles — a famous example of how straight strokes can produce a curved appearance.

### 21. Munchin — 棕櫚編織紋

- **Creator:** Maria Thomas
- **First published:** *Zentangle Primer Vol. 1*.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw two parallel curves to form a band.
  2. Within the band, draw a continuous diagonal weave — thick and thin alternating ribbons.
  3. The ribbons appear to interlock like a basket weave.
  4. Shade the under-ribbons darker.
- **Why it matters:** Munchin gives a strong "woven" texture often used inside Hollibaugh-like bands.

### 22. Flux — 流葉紋

- **Creator:** Rick Roberts & Maria Thomas
- **First published:** Featured in *Zentangle Primer Vol. 1* and many CZT courses.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw a leaf shape that bends like a flame.
  2. Add a vein curving along its length.
  3. Cluster many such leaves on a stem, each one curling differently.
  4. Vary the size; let some leaves overlap behind others.
- **Why it matters:** Flux is the workhorse organic tangle for foliage, feathers, and flowing edges. There are two official versions (Maria's and Rick's) with subtly different leaf shapes.

### 23. Chillon — 西庸城堡紋

- **Creator:** Maria Thomas (named for Switzerland's Château de Chillon)
- **First published:** Original Zentangle deck.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Lay down a grid.
  2. In each square, draw a small "+" with thick arms, leaving a tiny square at the centre.
  3. Connect the cross-arms of adjacent squares so the pattern reads as a continuous Greek key.
  4. Fill the negative squares black.
- **Why it matters:** Chillon turns a simple grid into a labyrinthine, architectural pattern.

### 24. Diva Dance — 女神之舞 / 舞動紋

- **Creator:** Maria Thomas (taught widely by Molly Hollibaugh)
- **First published:** Zentangle teaching DVDs and the Diva Dance video lesson.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw a long, asymmetric S-curve with a thick blunt end and a thin curling end.
  2. Beside it, draw another S-curve nesting against the first.
  3. Continue stacking the S-curves; each one mirrors and shifts the previous one.
  4. The result reads like waving fronds or a flickering flame.
- **Why it matters:** Diva Dance is a meditation in continuous motion — much loved for filling large open sections.

### 25. Bunzo — 邦佐紋 / 月珠紋

- **Creator:** Sandy Steen Bartholomew
- **First published:** *Yoga for Your Brain*.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw a soft round shape (a slightly squashed ball).
  2. Inside it, draw a series of nested half-moon curves all parallel to one edge.
  3. The result reads as a rolled, segmented pearl.
  4. Cluster many bunzos along a stem.
- **Why it matters:** Bunzo's nested arcs make any cluster look like a string of beads.

### 26. Cubine — 立方體紋

- **Creator:** Maria Thomas
- **First published:** Early Zentangle deck.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw a grid of squares.
  2. In each square, draw two diagonal lines from the corners, but stop them short of the centre — leaving a small square in the middle.
  3. Fill four of the resulting triangles black, leaving the diagonal opposites white.
  4. The pattern reads as a 3D cube tessellation.
- **Why it matters:** Cubine is the simplest tangle that produces a clear three-dimensional illusion.

### 27. Drawings — 線條紋 / 描畫紋 ("string" concept)

- **Creator:** Rick Roberts & Maria Thomas
- **First published:** Original Zentangle method; documented in *Zentangle Primer Vol. 1*.
- **How to draw it:** This is the "official" pencil-string concept that divides a tile into sections. Lightly draw a free-form pencil line across the tile — anything from a simple Z to a meandering scribble — to subdivide the square into 3–6 distinct regions. Each region becomes a canvas for one tangle.
- **Why it matters:** The string is the soul of Zentangle: it creates structure without dictating outcome.

## Section B — Sandy Steen Bartholomew Tangles

Sandy Steen Bartholomew, owner of *Beez in the Belfry* studio in New Hampshire and author of the influential *Yoga for Your Brain* series, contributed a number of widely-used tangles.

### 28. Zinging Static — see "Static" entry above (variant)

### 29. W2 — W2 結繩紋

- **Creator:** Sandy Steen Bartholomew
- **First published:** *Yoga for Your Brain*; subsequently archived on TanglePatterns.com.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw a column of "W" shapes stacked on top of each other.
  2. Beside that column, draw another column of "W" shapes offset by half so they interlock.
  3. The two columns weave together.
  4. Fill the small enclosed diamonds black.
- **Why it matters:** W2 produces a strong woven look from a single repeated letter.

### 30. Striping — 條紋紋 / 線陣紋

- **Creator:** Sandy Steen Bartholomew
- **First published:** *Yoga for Your Brain*.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Across a section, draw simple parallel lines of varying thickness and spacing.
  2. Optionally curve the lines to follow the contour of the section.
  3. Group thin and thick lines into bands of three to five.
- **Why it matters:** Striping is among the simplest tangles; useful as a calming filler that contrasts with busy neighbours.

### 31. Zig zag (Zander) — 茲格紋

- **Creator:** Sandy Steen Bartholomew (Zander is her son's name)
- **First published:** *Yoga for Your Brain*.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Across a section, draw a continuous zigzag line — sharp peaks alternating with sharp valleys.
  2. Beside it, draw another zigzag that mirrors the first, forming a row of diamonds between them.
  3. Add hatching or fill the diamonds black.
- **Why it matters:** Zig zag is the energetic counterpart to gentle Striping.

### 32. Nipa — 棕梠葉紋 / 椰葉紋

- **Creator:** Sandy Steen Bartholomew
- **First published:** *Yoga for Your Brain*.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw a long curving stem.
  2. From the stem, project pairs of pointed leaves like a palm frond.
  3. Each leaf is a long teardrop; alternate sides as you move along the stem.
  4. Fill alternate leaves with hatching or solid black.
- **Why it matters:** Nipa gives a tropical, fern-like band, especially useful in larger Phicops or Zendala work.

### 33. Verdigogh — 松針紋 / 翠針紋

- **Creator:** Sandy Steen Bartholomew
- **First published:** *Yoga for Your Brain*.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw a curving stem.
  2. From the stem, project clusters of short straight needles — five to seven needles per cluster.
  3. Each cluster fans out from a single point.
  4. Stagger clusters along the stem.
- **Why it matters:** Verdigogh is the canonical pine-bough tangle for winter or evergreen-themed tiles.

### 34. Tortuca — 龜殼紋

- **Creator:** Sandy Steen Bartholomew
- **First published:** *Yoga for Your Brain*.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw a hexagon.
  2. Inside it, draw a smaller hexagon.
  3. Repeat hexagons across the section in a tessellating honeycomb.
  4. Inside each hexagon, add a pattern (small cross, dot, or curve) like the markings on a tortoise shell.
- **Why it matters:** Tortuca is the foundation tangle for any honeycomb texture.

## Section C — Linda Farmer Tangles

Linda Farmer founded TanglePatterns.com in 2010 and is the keeper of the canonical online tangle archive. She has also created several tangles of her own.

### 35. Auras — 光環紋

- **Creator:** Linda Farmer (named the technique; the practice is older)
- **First published:** TanglePatterns.com; *The Book of Zentangle*.
- **How to draw it:** Auras is technically a *technique*, not a single pattern. Around any drawn shape, trace a thin offset line — its "aura." Add a second aura outside the first, then a third. The auras can be uniform or vary in spacing. Auras can also be drawn *inside* a shape, contracting toward the centre.
- **Why it matters:** Auras is one of the most versatile devices in the entire Zentangle vocabulary; it transforms a single line into a sense of glow or echo.

### 36. Sparkle — 閃光紋 / 留白紋

- **Creator:** Linda Farmer (technique attribution; widely used)
- **First published:** TanglePatterns.com; *Joy of Zentangle*.
- **How to draw it:** Sparkle is the deliberate technique of leaving a thin unbroken stripe of white through a tangle to suggest a highlight. While inking a long shape (a leaf, a curl, an aura), simply lift the pen for a heartbeat and skip 1–2 mm before resuming. The unbroken white reads as a glint of light.
- **Why it matters:** Sparkle is the cheapest, most powerful way to add dimension.

### 37. Arukas — 阿魯卡斯 / 雪花輻紋

- **Creator:** Mihoko Kurosaki, popularised through Linda Farmer's site
- **First published:** TanglePatterns.com.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw a small circle in the centre.
  2. From the circle, project an even number (8, 12, 16) of straight rays outward.
  3. Between each pair of adjacent rays, draw a triangular wedge that overlaps slightly into the next wedge.
  4. Add concentric rings of smaller wedges to the rays as you move outward.
- **Why it matters:** Arukas is one of the most popular intermediate tangles — radial, hypnotic, and impressive for the effort.

### 38. Rixty — 點線交錯紋

- **Creator:** Linda Farmer (often credited via her archive)
- **First published:** TanglePatterns.com.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw a square or rectangular grid.
  2. In every other cell, place a solid filled dot.
  3. In the remaining cells, draw a pair of crossing diagonal lines.
  4. The pattern reads as a checkerboard of dots and X's.
- **Why it matters:** Rixty is a clean, geometric filler good for backgrounds.

### 39. Quib — 奎布紋

- **Creator:** Linda Farmer
- **First published:** TanglePatterns.com.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw a vertical line.
  2. From it, project a series of thin upward-curving J-strokes, evenly spaced.
  3. Beside that, draw another vertical line and another set of J-strokes facing the opposite direction.
  4. The two columns interlock visually.
- **Why it matters:** Quib offers vertical rhythm without the heaviness of Striping.

## Section D — Other CZT-Contributed Tangles

### 40. Amaze — 迷宮紋

- **Creator:** Carole Ohl, CZT
- **First published:** TanglePatterns.com; widely featured in CZT classes.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Pencil a square.
  2. Starting from one corner, draw a continuous line that spirals inward, making a 90° turn at each step — like a maze without walls.
  3. The line ends at the centre of the square.
  4. Repeat in adjacent squares; alternate the starting corner so the spirals interlock.
- **Why it matters:** Amaze is the canonical "labyrinth" tangle, very satisfying to draw slowly.

### 41. Wud — 樹皮紋 / 木紋

- **Creator:** Karry Heun, CZT
- **First published:** TanglePatterns.com.
- **How to draw it:**
  1. Draw a row of vertical wavy lines, like the cross-section of stacked planks.
  2. Inside each plank, draw concentric oval rings — like tree-ring grain.
  3. Optionally darken one edge of each plank to suggest separation.
- **Why it matters:** Wud is the go-to texture for any wooden, bark-like, or grain effect.

## How to Read This Dictionary

Each entry is a **starting point**, not a final answer. Zentangle is a living tradition. When you see "draw a row of half-moons" you should expect:

1. **Variations are encouraged.** Most tangles have official variants (Cadent has at least three documented variations; Flux exists in both Maria's version and Rick's version). Your own slight differences are part of the tradition, not deviations from it.
2. **The order matters.** Zentangle teaches that you should *always ink in pen, never sketch in pencil first* (except for the string and shading). This forces you to commit and trust the process. Each verbal step in the descriptions above is a single pen stroke or a small group of strokes; pause between groups.
3. **Shading comes last.** After all the line work is finished, then bring in the 2B pencil and tortillon for shading.
4. **Mistakes become features.** A line that wanders is not erased — it becomes the seed of a new tangle. This is one of the deepest lessons of the practice.

A typical study tile uses three to six tangles, each in its own section of the string. Beginners should master Crescent Moon, Hollibaugh, Printemps, Tipple, Florz, and Knightsbridge before branching out — those six alone produce hundreds of beautiful tile combinations.

## Where to Find More

- **TanglePatterns.com** — Linda Farmer's archive at https://tanglepatterns.com is the canonical online reference. As of 2026 it documents well over 1,800 tangles, each with a verbal step-out and most with attribution. The "Tangle Patterns String Theory" section also archives strings for daily practice.
- **The Zentangle Primer, Volumes 1 and 2** by Rick Roberts and Maria Thomas — the founders' own published reference.
- **One Zentangle a Day** by Beckah Krahula — a 6-week daily program; excellent for new practitioners.
- **Yoga for Your Brain** and its sequels by Sandy Steen Bartholomew — playful, accessible introductions.
- **Joy of Zentangle** by Sandy Bartholomew, Suzanne McNeill, and Marie Browning — broad survey with many CZT contributions.
- **ZenAgain** — the official Zentangle annual conference; many new tangles debut here each year.

The dictionary above is an entry point. The full practice rewards years of exploration, and every tile you draw will quietly add new variants of your own to your personal vocabulary.
