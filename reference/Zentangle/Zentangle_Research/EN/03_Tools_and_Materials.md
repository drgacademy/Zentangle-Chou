# 03 — Tools and Materials

## Why the Tools Matter

Zentangle was designed around a deliberately small toolkit. Rick Roberts and Maria Thomas, the founders, chose each tool not for its prestige but for what it removes from the artist's mind. A Micron pen does not skip; a 2B pencil does not smear unpredictably; an official tile fits exactly in the palm. When the materials are reliable, the practitioner stops thinking about *whether the line will work* and starts thinking only about *the next stroke*. This is the philosophical reason behind the consistent kit — it lowers the cost of error and lets the meditative rhythm take over.

Below is the full official starter kit, plus a few substitutes for beginners who want to try the practice before investing.

---

## 1. The Pen — Sakura Pigma Micron 01

The **Sakura Pigma Micron 01** (black, 0.25 mm tip) is the canonical Zentangle pen. Its qualities are:

- **Archival pigment ink.** Unlike dye-based inks, Pigma ink is made of suspended pigment particles. It is rated archival — it will not fade for decades when stored away from direct sunlight, and it will not yellow.
- **Waterproof and bleed-resistant.** Once dry (within seconds), the ink does not run when you shade with a pencil and tortillon over it. This is critical: in Zentangle you ink first and shade second.
- **Consistent flow.** The plastic nib delivers a uniform 0.25 mm line whether you draw quickly or slowly, vertically or upside-down. There is no pressure dependency, which means a beginner's wobbly hand and a master's steady hand produce the same line weight.
- **No solvent smell, acid-free, pH-neutral.** Safe in classrooms and around children.

### The Micron family of sizes

While the **01** is the workhorse, the full set is useful as practice deepens:

| Pen | Tip width | Typical use |
|-----|-----------|-------------|
| Pigma Micron 005 | 0.20 mm | Fine detail inside small tangles, hatching on Bijou tiles |
| Pigma Micron 01 | 0.25 mm | The default — line work for almost every tangle |
| Pigma Micron 03 | 0.35 mm | Bolder borders, frames, and the Zentangle "string" |
| Pigma Micron 05 | 0.45 mm | Filling solid black areas (e.g. Knightsbridge) |
| Pigma Micron 08 | 0.50 mm | Heavy outlines, large-format tiles, Phicops rectangles |

A practical purchase is the 01 alone for the first six months, then add 005 and 08 once you start experimenting with contrast.

## 2. The Pencil — General's 2B Graphite

The official Zentangle pencil is the **General's Semi-Hex No. 498 in 2B**, made in Jersey City, New Jersey by the General Pencil Company (founded 1889). Why specifically 2B?

- **Softness for shading.** A 2B graphite core deposits enough particles to create soft grey gradients quickly, without requiring heavy pressure.
- **Hardness for control.** Unlike 4B or 6B pencils, 2B does not over-smudge. The graphite stays put under a tortillon long enough for you to plan the gradient.
- **Cedar barrel.** General's pencils are cased in California incense cedar, which sharpens cleanly without splintering.

The pencil is used for two things only in Zentangle: drawing the **string** (the loose pencil border that subdivides the tile into sections) and **shading** the inked tangles afterward. It is never used for the tangles themselves.

---

## 3. The Tortillon (Paper Stump)

A **tortillon** is a tightly rolled cone of soft grey paper, about 8–12 cm long, with a pointed tip. It is used to push graphite around the tile so that the pencil's grey transitions become smooth gradients.

**How to use it:**
1. Lay down a small patch of 2B graphite at the edge of a shape.
2. Hold the tortillon at a low angle, almost flat to the paper.
3. Sweep from the dark edge outward into the lighter area. Do not press hard — let the tortillon's own friction drag the graphite.
4. Rotate the tortillon every few strokes to expose a clean side.

**Tortillon vs. blending stump.** People confuse the two. A **tortillon** is rolled paper, hollow inside, and slightly soft — best for small tiles and delicate gradients. A **blending stump** is solid compressed paper, pointed at both ends, and firmer — better for large drawings. For 3.5-inch Zentangle tiles, the tortillon is preferred because its smaller diameter gives finer control.

When the tortillon's tip becomes too saturated with graphite, you can clean it by rubbing the tip on a scrap of sandpaper or by simply unrolling the paper at the tip to expose fresh layers.

## 4. The Tile

The **official Zentangle tile** is a 3.5-inch × 3.5-inch (89 mm) square of fine art paper. The size was chosen because it can be completed in roughly 15–20 minutes — long enough for a meditative session, short enough to finish in one sitting.

### Tile varieties

| Tile | Size | Color | Surface | Notes |
|------|------|-------|---------|-------|
| **Original (Italian White)** | 3.5" × 3.5" | Off-white | Smooth, slightly absorbent | The classic. Sometimes called "Fox & Geese" after the watermark on the original Fabriano paper. |
| **Renaissance** | 3.5" × 3.5" | Tan / warm beige | Same as Original | Used with brown Micron + white charcoal pencil for a sepia look. |
| **Black** | 3.5" × 3.5" | Deep black | Smooth | Use with white Sakura Gelly Roll or white charcoal pencil. |
| **Bijou** | 2" × 2" | White | Same as Original | Miniature tile for daily practice or jewellery. |
| **Zendala** | 4.5" round | White | Same as Original | Circular tile for mandala-style compositions. |
| **Phicops** | 7" × 3" (approx) | White | Same as Original | Long rectangle, named after a fossil trilobite — good for panoramic strings. |
| **Apprentice** | 4.5" × 4.5" | White | Slightly heavier | Larger tile designed for children and classroom use. |

### Paper specifications

The official tiles are made in Italy by Fabriano (and now also by other mills under licence). The paper is:

- **Cotton-content rag paper** — typically 50% or higher cotton, which gives a soft, slightly springy surface.
- **Acid-free and archival** — will not yellow.
- **Deckled edges** — the slightly torn, feathered edges are not a defect; they are a signature of fine art paper. Deckle edges signal that this is a finished art object, not a page from a notebook.
- **Slightly absorbent surface** — the Micron ink sits in the surface fibres, drying instantly without bleeding sideways. Pencil graphite also catches on the gentle tooth of the paper, giving the tortillon something to push.

## 5. White Highlights — Gelly Roll and White Charcoal

For **Black tiles** and for adding sparkle on **Renaissance tiles**, two white tools are used:

- **Sakura Gelly Roll Classic 08 White.** A water-based gel ink pen that lays down opaque white. On black tiles it functions exactly as the Micron does on white tiles — same line discipline, same archival quality. Allow a few extra seconds to dry; do not rub or it will smear.
- **General's White Charcoal Pencil.** A compressed white chalk in pencil form. Used with a tortillon (a different tortillon — keep your white and black ones separate) to create soft white gradients on tan and black tiles. Some practitioners prefer a white Conté pencil or a white pastel pencil; all behave similarly.

## 6. Substitutes for Beginners

You do not need the official kit to start. The practice is more important than the brand. Reasonable substitutes:

- **Pen.** Any waterproof, archival fine-liner: Faber-Castell Pitt Artist Pen (size S), Staedtler Pigment Liner 0.3, Copic Multiliner 0.3, or Uni Pin 0.3. Avoid ballpoints (too inconsistent) and gel pens (too wet) for line work on white paper.
- **Pencil.** Any 2B drawing pencil: Staedtler Mars Lumograph 2B, Faber-Castell 9000 2B, Mitsubishi Hi-Uni 2B.
- **Paper.** Any heavyweight (at least 200 gsm) smooth or vellum-finish drawing paper, cut to 9 cm squares. A Strathmore 400 series sketchbook works well. Mixed-media paper from any art store is also fine.
- **Tortillon.** Inexpensive packs are sold at any art shop for under USD 3.
- **White pen.** Any opaque white gel pen (Uni-ball Signo Broad UM-153 is excellent).

The main thing to preserve is the **square format** and the **fine archival ink**. The exact brand is secondary.

## 7. Storage and Display

Finished tiles accumulate quickly — one tile per day means 30 tiles per month. Consider:

- **Tile albums.** Several manufacturers (including Zentangle Inc. itself) sell small portfolio albums with 3.5-inch pockets. These keep the tiles flat, dust-free, and protected from sunlight.
- **Mosaic mounting.** A Zentangle "mosaic" is a grid of nine tiles (or 16, or 25) arranged together to form a larger composition. Tiles can be mounted on archival board with photo corners — this is reversible and does not damage the deckled edges.
- **Frames.** A standard 4-inch × 4-inch shadow box frame fits a single tile beautifully and lets the deckle edge cast a small shadow.
- **Avoid sleeves with PVC** — over years they can release plasticisers that interact with pen ink. Use polypropylene or polyester pockets.

---

## 8. A Practical Starter Shopping List

For someone beginning Zentangle today, the minimum responsible kit is:

1. One Sakura Pigma Micron 01, black
2. One General's 2B pencil (or any 2B)
3. One tortillon (paper stump)
4. A small pack of official Original tiles (commonly sold in packs of 36) — or, as a substitute, a Strathmore 400 mixed-media pad cut to 3.5-inch squares
5. A small sharpener
6. *Optional:* one white Sakura Gelly Roll 08 if you also bought black tiles

Total cost, including official tiles: roughly USD 35–45. Substitute kit: under USD 15.

This is a deliberately modest investment. The practice itself, repeated daily, is what produces the result — not the tools.
